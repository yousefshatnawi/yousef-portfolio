About

Before writing a single line of code, I think about structure. Where should state live? What's the smallest, most composable unit here? How will this module scale when the requirements change next sprint? That mental model — shaped by building real enterprise systems under real constraints — is what separates a component that works from one that lasts.

At Unlimited Innovation, I work inside large-scale Angular codebases serving government and enterprise clients. A significant part of that work is refactoring — taking components that have grown into hundreds of lines and decomposing them into smaller, testable, reusable pieces. This is not glamorous work. But it's the work that makes a codebase sustainable, and it's the work I'm good at.

I started my career in Help Desk before moving into software development. That time taught me something most developers miss: users don't experience your architecture, they experience what breaks. That perspective shapes every interface I build — I optimize for the person on the other side of the screen, not just the reviewer reading the pull request.
